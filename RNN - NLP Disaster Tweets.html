Executive Summary¶

This project explored recurrent neural networks (RNN) and their ability to process time series data. The project goal was to binary classify whether a tweet were about a disaster. Processing tweet information requires natural language processing to convert the strings into interpretable sequences. The dataset was soured from the Kaggle 'Natural Language Processing with Disaster Tweets' Competition.

The project performed standard exploratory analysis before exploring word embedding in depth. This involved processing word embedding data, creating corpos specific vocabularies, and converting tweets to padded word sequences based on word index.

The first project focus compared the generalized word embeddings of GloVe to learned, application-specific embeddings. The application-specific embeddings performed better, quickly fitting the training data and achieving 0.706 test accuracy. The generalized embeddings trained much slower and performed worse on test data with 0.635 accuracy.

Subsequent testing simplified the embeddings, reducing the embedding dimensions and number of RNN units. It was found the initial model of 100 embedding dimensions and 128 RNN units may be overly complex. The rapid rate of training and reasonable validation accuracy was maintained at 16 embedding dimensions and 16 RNN units. Models surprisingly showed limited overfitting, maintaining consistent accuracy despite long training epochs.

A final comparison evaluated the reduced complexity SimpleRNN model, the basis of training thus far, to LSTM and GRU methods. While LSTM was slightly slower to train (trailing by perhaps 1 epoch) and GRU seemed to have a 10 epoch delay before effective training, both outperformed the SimpleRNN architecture in validation accuracy. However, after 50 epochs the methods performed effectivey equal as SimpleRNN improved and the other methods began overfitting. Since the longest tweet sequence was 31 words, accuracy in LSTM and GRU is likely to improve more dramatically for longer sequences.

Across all testing, it was found that even simple heuristic based models with custom embeddings and a complex single layer RNN can reliably capture training data without dramatic runtime. However, these models can likely be simplified, perhaps instead using computation resources for additional layers rather than additional embedding dimensions or weights. Unsurprisingly, LSTM and GRU ourperform SimpleRNN, though for short tweet sequences the improvements are only marginal.

Future work could consider further modifications to the RNN architecture, including convolutional layers to structure sequences in the RNN or stacking RNN layers to reveal more complex relationships. Other word embedding methods such as BERT could add further resolution to the word embedding process. Additional pre-processing to replace typos or other word errors would also improve model performance. While this project focused on binary classification, the RNN framework can be applied to applications such as tweet sentiment, summarization, or more complex multi-class classification work.

While the technology of RNN appears complex, its implementation in building models is surprisingly simple. RNN's combination with NLP approaches opens an entire world of data science applications.

Representing Words Numerically¶

There are a two core problems in natural language processing: 1) words need to be encoded as numbers, 2) those encodings ideally capture the context/sentiment/meaning of a word.

One-hot encoding word presence¶

Consider one-hot encoding of words. Perhaps we have two similar sentences: "I like apples" and "I like bananas". Say we have an input array of all possible words, with 0 indicating a word is absent and 1 when a word is present. For example, [1,1,1,0] could represent "I like apples", and [1,1,0,1] could represent "I like bananas". If we were evaluating sentiment, perhaps our network could notice the similarities between these samples group them together.

However, consider "I don't like apples" and "I like apples, don't you?". Both samples contain the same words and therefore very similar encodings. However, the context of the words are lost taken without order or meaning.

Further, consider the below word count for our 23,537 distinct words, 18,217 of which occur only once.

Other word presence methods like Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) calculates the frequency a word is used in a document/tweet and the frequency a word is used across a corpus of all documents/tweets. Words used frequently in one document but not frequently in the corpus score a high TF-IDF, indicating that word helps distinguish that document. This helps reduce the effect of common words, though this process too removes context from the words. A book about apple the fruit and Apple the company may rank "apple" similarly high in TF-IDF.

So how do we contextualize words in general?

Text Embedding - Representing Word Meaning as a Vector¶

Note that one-hot encoding does not identify similarities between words. In the previous example, a model may learn 'apples' and 'bananas' are similar if there are training examples that use them similarly. In the context of disasters, "WARNING: TSUNAMI, SEEK SHELTER" is functionally the same as "WARNING: EARTHQUAKE/TORNADO...". These word similarities between tsunami and earthquake are captured using text embeddings.

Imagine yourself in a library looking for a book about World War II. Perhaps you are in the children's section, which hopefully does not have books about World War II. Maybe you look around and locate the history section, then see books about World War I, then books about World War II.

Like organizing books in a libary by subject, imagine placing every word in the human language on an infinitely spanning bookshelf. On the bookshelf, perhaps there is a region of adjectives spanning [-100, -50], verbs from [-25, 25], and nouns from [50, 100]. Within the region of nouns, perhaps there is a section for animals from [75, 80], or fruits from [85, 90]. On this bookshelf, like the Dewey decimal system, every word is assigned a location. "Orange" is [85.2], "lemon" is [85.3], "verbose" is [-75]. Similar words are assigned similar values.

These text embeddings aren't limited to a single dimension. For example, perhaps there is a dimension describing object color, or size, or more abstract qualities like "associated with childhood".

General text embeddings range from 50 to 200 dimensions, locating every word in the human language somewhere within this abstract space, grouping them close to similar words. Some models of text embedding can even capture abstract relationships numerically, such as "king - man + woman = queen".

Interestingly, neural networks learn these text embeddings to assign each word its location. Several pre-trained text embeddings are available for use:

  • GloVe (Global Vectors for Word Representation) - Uses word-word co-occurence to understand meaning. For example, 'ice' occurs more often with 'solid' than it does with 'gas'. This creates a globally based embedding capturing many types of relationships. (https://nlp.stanford.edu/projects/glove/)
  • Word2Vec - Means of converting words to dense vector representations. Context words are input and the model is trained to predict the output term. (https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/blog/2021/07/word2vec-for-word-embeddings-a-beginners-guide/)
  • BERT - Model which can develop contextual embeddings for the same word. For example, apple the fruit and the company should have very different embeddings. Given a sentence context, BERT may use different embeddings for the same word to improve performance. (https://huggingface.co/docs/transformers/en/model_doc/distilbert)

In all these cases, embeddings result from neural network training. As such, we can also train our own embeddings custom to our application.

Text Embedding Comparison + Building the RNN¶

The below code constructs two models for comparison:

  1. Model 1 uses GloVe-provided text embeddings which capture the general similarity/semantics of each word. The 6B token dataset, including the 100 dimension embedding, is downloadable here: https://nlp.stanford.edu/data/glove.6B.zip
  2. Model 2 learns its own text embeddings in the exclusive context of classifying if tweets are about disasters.

Both models are based on a relatively simple neural network. Text embedding is performed as pre-processing step, either initialized from GloVe or learned like any other parameter in a neural network. The data is passed to a 128 unit RNN, where each unit develops its own weights during training. The output of those units is passed to a single dense layer before being sent to a sigmoid classifier for output.

Detailed explanation of incorporating pre-built text embeddings.¶

Unfortunately we cannot simply load our text data into our neural networks. Remember is we aren't learning embeddings from scratch, we need to extract them from a learned dataset. To achieve this, we need to:

  1. Isolate the words relevant to our analysis and tokenize them into an index - We want to assign each relevant word an index we will use to build our look-up matrix. This process should prioritize frequently used words. The result is our word_index.
  2. Extract all possible embeddings from the pre-built embeddings - Pre-built text embeddings like GloVe are easily downloaded from the internet. These files are formatted like a .csv, with their word in the first column and their subsequent embedding dimensions. For example, the first entry may be [a, 0, 1, ...] up to 100 embedding dimensions. We load these into our embedding_index.
  3. Record the embeddings for words within our specified vocab_size - Since our word_index is organized by frequency, we look up each word in our embedding_index to isolate their embedding_vector. We then record that embedding_vector in our embedding_matrix. Recall we don't need all words, just a reasonable vocabulary size prioritizing words used most frequently. As such, repeat this process for our specified vocab_size.

The project implements these steps below:

# Tokenizer - Index each word that appears in tweet content based on word frequency
tokenizer = Tokenizer(num_words=vocab_size, oov_token="<OOV>")
tokenizer.fit_on_texts(df_train.text) 

# Vocabulary word index
word_index = tokenizer.word_index

# # Load GloVe embeddings
embedding_index = {}                                     # Initialize dict
with open('glove.6B.100d.txt', encoding='utf-8') as embeddings:
    for line in embeddings:                              # For each line
        values = line.split()                            # Split word and coeffs into a list
        word = values[0]                                 # Extract word
        coefs = np.asarray(values[1:], dtype='float32')  # Extract embeddings
        embedding_index[word] = coefs                    # Write coeffs to dict

# Create the embedding matrix - What is actually used in model
embedding_matrix = np.zeros((vocab_size, embedding_dim))
for word, i in word_index.items():                       # For each word
    if i < vocab_size:                                   # If room for more words
        embedding_vector = embedding_index.get(word)     # Get vector from index based on GloVe
        if embedding_vector is not None:                 
            embedding_matrix[i] = embedding_vector       # Write vector to matrix assuming it exists
    else:
        break                                            # Break early if vocab_size is met

Preparing Training Data¶

Now that we have an embeddings matrix, we need to convert our text strings into something our models can interpret. Using our word_index, all tweets can now be represented using those indexes. For example, "I like apples" becomes [3, 35, 100].

However, our neural network is expecting a fixed length input. We achieve this by padding our sequences at the end. Our longest tweet is 31 words long, so we will use a maximum length of 40 for good measure. With padding, "I like apples" becomes [3, 35, 100, 0, 0, 0...] out to 40 words.

These padded sequences become the input to our model. We perform the process on our training and testing data before it is fed to our models.

Building the model¶

Finally we can build our two models where the only difference occurs in the Embedding layer.

In the GloVe model, we use our pre-built embedding_matrix and do not update the weights during training. In the learned embeddings models, the embeddings are randomly intialized and updated through backpropagation during model training. These learned embeddings act as learning parameters, providing an additional 500,000 parameters for the model to adjust.

    '''
    start = time.time()
    clone_model = models.clone_model(model)
    log = clone_model.fit(train, label, 
                              epochs = epochs, 
                              validation_split=0.10, 
                              batch_size = batch_size, 
                              verbose=0)
    duration = time.time()-start
    accuracy = log.history['accuracy'][-1]
    val_accuracy = log.history['val_accuracy'][-1]
    ## Print results
    print(f"  Duration:{round(duration,1)}s, Accuracy:{round(accuracy,3)}, Val Accuracy: {round(val_accuracy,3)}")
    return clone_model, log

## Epoch Plot Function
def epoch_plots(log,suptitle=""):
    '''
    Plots accuracy, precision, recall and loss from model log.
    '''   
    params = [log.history['accuracy'],
              log.history.get('precision', []),
              log.history.get('recall', []),
              log.history['loss']]
    val_params = [log.history['val_accuracy'],
                  log.history.get('val_precision', []),
                  log.history.get('val_recall', []),
                  log.history['val_loss']]
    labels = ['Accuracy', 
              'Precision',
              'Recall',
              'Binary Cross Entropy Loss']
    epochs = range(1, len(log.history['accuracy']) + 1)  # Epoch numbers

    # Plotting
    fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 4, figsize=(20, 4))
    for ax, param, val_param, label in zip(axes.flatten(),params,val_params,labels):
        ax.plot(epochs, param, label='Training')
        ax.plot(epochs, val_param, label='Validation')
        ax.set_title(label+' vs Epochs')
        ax.set_xlabel('Epochs')
        ax.set_ylabel(label)
        ax.set_ylim([0,1])
        ax.legend(loc='lower left')
        ax.grid(True)   
    fig.suptitle(suptitle, fontweight="bold")
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

Conclusion¶

This project seeked to use RNN in a binary classification application using NLP.

The project considered the effects of learning text embeddings compared to importing fixed, general text embeddings from GloVe. Unsurprisingly, learned text embeddings performed better for this specific task than general embeddings due to the additional parameters in the model. This however also led to some overfitting, though the learned embeddings validation accuracy performed better than the general GloVe model. An ideal approach may use general embeddings to initialize the embeddings, then allow the model to update weights specific to the task. In this relatively small application however, the model quickly learned embeddings within less than 10 training epochs on only 6,000 training samples.

Next the model tuned hyperparameters, experimenting with the dimensions of learned text embeddings and the number of RNN units in the RNN layer. Since initial testing indicated strong performance and very fast training, both the embedding dimensions and RNN units were reduced to simplify the model while hopefully maintaining accuracy. It was found that the embedding dimensions could be reduced from 100 (based on the GloVe embeddings) down to 16 without penalty to accuracy and actually reduced overfitting. Similarly, the number of RNN units were reduced from 128 to 16 with similar effect. The model can likely be simplified further, though this was not confirmed during the project.

Finally the simplified model compared performance to alternative RNN structures. While all work had first used the SimpleRNN structure, most RNN now use LSTM or GRU. Again, due to the simplicity of the application (and seemingly large influence of learned embeddings), I did not expect dramatic model improvements. However, while both alternative models trained slightly slower (with GRU plateuing for several epochs before learning), both offered higher validation accuracy without as much overfitting.

Unfortunately many of the experiments did not dramatically improve performance, though they did demonstrate how a simpler model could perform similarly. It is likely the intial model was sufficiently complex to represent the application.

Future work may consider other modifications to the model, such as using convolution layers as a mean to structure sequences fed into the RNN. Like stacking convolution layers, stacking RNN layers may also reveal more complex sequence relationships. This project focused on binary classification, but a similar RNN framework could use unsupervised learning for tweet classification, sentiment, or summarizing fed text. While custom embeddings performed best in this project, it appears BERT or DistilBERT based on Google transformer technology is the industry standard.

While the technology of RNN appears complex, its implementation in building models is surprisingly simple. RNN's combination with NLP approaches opens an entire world of data science applications.
